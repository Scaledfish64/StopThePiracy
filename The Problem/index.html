The Problem

Software piracy is far too easy and makes buying the actual product look like a bad choice. Illegal websites make piracy simple and nearly impossible to turn down. When people are provided with an easy and free route, they always take it over the pricey one. It's just natural to do so even if it's morally wrong. But sometimes, it's diffrent. An avid software Pirater said this:

 “If only it were available to buy legally and easily, I would” (Keep).

Sometimes, it's not even laziness; it's the only option. For example, when a console is officially pulled from the market, its games are also out of the market. If you want to play that game now, you would have to own both the game and the console which may cause more and more users to illegally play the game.

Above, we can see 343 Industries (Owned by Microsft) Sunsetting certain parts of the Halo franchise ("Xbox 360 Only").

But we have to look at this situation from both sides.
What could possibly be making developers shut down old and beloved software/games instead of just keeping them running in the background? Wouldn't that still let players who have the game still be able to play it? Jana Brnakova makes an analogy that helps understand the situation:

 “Imagine maintaining a house built decades ago. The wiring might be outdated, the plumbing a mystery, and spare parts nearly impossible to find. That's the challenge developers face with keeping alive the legacy code written for older technology behind decommissioned games.” (Brnakova).

Even if you had the right tools to maintain the house, you would still need to pay for the water, electricity, and so on. For games, this is maintaining servers as keeping these large super-computers up is no easy task. Developers will often kill these Servers and use them for newer games. Killing the servers usually make the game unplayable, or partially unplayable.
